Women with PhDs

Authors:

Danting Gao,    Eric Rawn,    Joy Doong

The reality that women face significant barriers in the working world has been an unfortunately long-term aspect of our society. On the whole, women are paid less for equal work, have greater difficulty finding work, and are expected more often to shoulder the responsibilities of family care , for which they often have to adjust their careers. The effects of COVID-19 have only exacerbated these structural inequalities against women in the workplace.

Academia is no exception to these structural forces. We used the 2017 NSF Survey of Doctorate Recipients, a survey that asks more than 120,000 Doctoral Graduates about their career paths and priorities, to bring a quantitative approach to studying the experiences of women in academia. We hope these visualizations will bring greater clarity to the pathways of women in academia, their priorities, and the structural barriers they face in succeeding.

The Survey of Doctorate Recipients (SDR) , conducted by the National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics (NCSES) within the National Science Foundation, provides data on the characteristics of science, engineering, and health (SEH) doctorate degree holders. It samples individuals who have earned an SEH research doctoral degree from a U.S. academic institution and are less than 76 years of age. The SDR provides data useful in assessing the supply and characteristics of the nation's SEH doctorates employed in educational institutions, private industry, and professional organizations, as well as in federal, state, and local governments.

Population size: Approximately 1,103,200 individuals

Sample size: A total of 124,580 individuals

    PhD Recipients by Race


    Here, we zoom in specifically on Race to see that Doctoral Students of Asian descent have seen the greatest increase. It's important to recognize that even though we see total numbers of women increasing, those gains are not equally distributed racially. While the rise in Women who recieve PhDs is something to celebrate, we should remain cognizant of how racial inequalities impact gender equity.

  • Use the radio buttons to switch between Academic and Industry dataset
  • Use the "Plot By" drop-down to switch between absolute salary values and gap percentage values
  • Use the "Sort By" drop-down to sort the list order
  • The calculation of Average Salary Gap (%) is: (male salary - female salary) / male salary * 100%

    • Here we can see the wage gap of women with PhDs by industry. From the graph, it's clear that men make more than women in nearly every field, with the only significant wage gap that favors women present in pre-college teaching. In some fields, women are making less than $20,000 less annually than their male counterparts. This visualization effectively highlights which areas should be the highest priority for reform, either from Institutions themselves or through legislation.

    Family Responsibilities by Gender




    These two charts reveal who has to shoulder the responsibilities of family care. We can see from the top chart that a much greater number of women choose to work part time due to family responsibilities than men, significantly around the time when many choose to raise younger children, around 35-45 years old. The bottom chart shows that as a percentage of the total, women choose more often across age groups to work part time due to family commitments.


    This project examined the role of women in academia across lines of race, sector, wage, and priorities. We found that while recently women have been making strong gains for gender parity on the whole, for specific sectors and for minorities such as Black and Latinx women, gender parity has a long way to go. For nearly every sector, men make significantly more than their female counterparts. For all the stark structural issues this data presents, it importantly leaves out the many women who wanted to obtain a PhD but were unable to due to structural barriers. Especially in sectors like computer science where men still outnumber women by a wide margin, it is clear that women are being recruited into programs less, actively pushed out of programs more, and are not given positions as lucrative or as often. We also showed that women value the social impact of their work more highly than men and prioritize their employment benefits more highly, perhaps due to their greater responsibilities associated with family and child care. In working towards gender parity within the academy and in industry, understanding the unique structural challenges women face in their careers is key to shaping effective policy and reform.
