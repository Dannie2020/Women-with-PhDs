Women with PhDs

Authors:

Danting Gao,    Eric Rawn,    Joy Doong

The reality that women face significant barriers in the working world has been an unfortunately long-term aspect of our society. On the whole, women are paid less for equal work, have greater difficulty finding work, and are expected more often to shoulder the responsibilities of family care , for which they often have to adjust their careers. The effects of COVID-19 have only exacerbated these structural inequalities against women in the workplace.

Academia is no exception to these structural forces. We used the 2017 NSF Survey of Doctorate Recipients, a survey that asks more than 120,000 Doctoral Graduates about their career paths and priorities, to bring a quantitative approach to studying the experiences of women in academia. We hope these visualizations will bring greater clarity to the pathways of women in academia, their priorities, and the structural barriers they face in succeeding.

The Survey of Doctorate Recipients (SDR) , conducted by the National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics (NCSES) within the National Science Foundation, provides data on the characteristics of science, engineering, and health (SEH) doctorate degree holders. It samples individuals who have earned an SEH research doctoral degree from a U.S. academic institution and are less than 76 years of age. The SDR provides data useful in assessing the supply and characteristics of the nation's SEH doctorates employed in educational institutions, private industry, and professional organizations, as well as in federal, state, and local governments.

Population size: Approximately 1,103,200 individuals

Sample size: A total of 124,580 individuals

    Importance of Benefits By Gender and Sector



    This visualization shows the intersection of gender and sector on Benefits Satisfaction. While the proportion of those "Very Satisfied" with their benefits is consistent with the overall count of men and women, the proportion of those who are "Somewhat" or "Very Dissatisfied" skews significantly towards women. This visualization also reveals that although academia is on the whole reaching gender parity, the number of women in Industry is still significantly lower. These findings are consistent with our earlier assumptions - that women are more likely to be shouldered with childcare responsibilities and may prioritize the flexibility offered by academia over an industry job.

    Family Responsibilities by Gender




    These two charts reveal who has to shoulder the responsibilities of family care. We can see from the top chart that a much greater number of women choose to work part time due to family responsibilities than men, significantly around the time when many choose to raise younger children, around 35-45 years old. The bottom chart shows that as a percentage of the total, women choose more often across age groups to work part time due to family commitments.


    This project examined the role of women in academia across lines of race, sector, wage, and priorities. We found that while recently women have been making strong gains for gender parity on the whole, for specific sectors gender parity has a long way to go. For nearly every sector, men make significantly more than their female counterparts. For all the stark structural issues this data presents, it importantly leaves out the many women who wanted to obtain a PhD but were unable to due to structural barriers. Especially in sectors like computer science where men still outnumber women by a wide margin, it is clear that women are being recruited into programs less, actively pushed out of programs more, and are not given positions as lucrative or as often.
